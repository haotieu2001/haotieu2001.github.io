Every dashboard, every fraud model, every regulatory report a bank files with the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) starts the same way: as a raw row of data sitting in some source system, with no idea yet that anyone needs it. ❯ Short version (read this first)

The data engineering lifecycle has five stages — Generation → Storage → Ingestion → Transformation → Serving — with storage underlying every other stage rather than sitting in a strict sequence.

Six undercurrents — security, data management, DataOps, data architecture, orchestration, and software engineering — run through all five stages simultaneously and are what separate a fragile pipeline from a professional one.

Architecture and tool choice are decisions made under real constraints (team size, cost, time-to-market, reversibility) — the book's stance is deliberately "it depends," not "always use X."

• Good architecture — flexible, loosely coupled, reversible, and built to fail gracefully, not a single "best" diagram you copy from a vendor's slide.

• Good pipelines — move data with the right latency (batch vs. streaming), transform it into a model people can trust (star schema, Data Vault, or a wide table), and serve it without ever letting training-time and production-time views of the same fact disagree.

Together: data engineering is the practice of building the boring, reliable plumbing — governed, secure, and cost-aware — that every dashboard, report, and machine learning model quietly depends on and almost never gets credit for.

A data pipeline's latency is how long it takes data to become usable after it is generated. Idempotent means running an operation twice produces the same result as running it once — a property every reliable pipeline needs. 01. What Is Data Engineering?

The discipline that turns scattered, unreliable raw data into something trustworthy enough to build on.

Data engineering is the discipline of designing, building, and maintaining the systems that collect, move, store, and prepare data so that other people and other systems can use it. It is infrastructure work in service of information: a data engineer's job is to make sure that by the time a dataset reaches an analyst's dashboard, a data scientist's notebook, or a machine learning model's feature pipeline, it is complete, correctly shaped, reasonably fresh, and something people can actually trust.

That framing matters because it draws a clean line between data engineering and the roles that sit downstream of it. Data scientists and analysts are primarily consumers of what data engineering produces — they ask questions of the data, build models on it, and turn it into decisions. Data engineers build and operate the plumbing that makes those questions answerable in the first place. A brilliant model trained on inconsistent, late, or silently corrupted data will produce brilliant-looking nonsense; the reliability of everything downstream is bounded by the quality of the engineering underneath it.

The role itself has moved through a few distinct eras. In the 1990s and 2000s it was largely the province of database administrators and ETL developers, hand-writing scripts to move data between operational systems and a single relational data warehouse. The 2010s brought the "big data" era — Hadoop, MapReduce, and a wave of engineers who spent most of their time managing distributed clusters just to keep the lights on. The current era, often called the modern data stack, is SQL- and cloud-centric: managed warehouses and lakehouses, pay-as-you-go ingestion tools, and transformation frameworks that let a much smaller team do what used to require a large infrastructure org. The tools changed; the underlying job — making data usable — did not.

Data Engineering

The engineering discipline focused on the full lifecycle of data — its generation, storage, movement, transformation, and delivery — with an emphasis on reliability, security, scalability, and cost, rather than on the analysis performed with the data itself.

Example: An online retailer's checkout service writes order events to an application database. A data engineer builds the pipeline that captures those events, lands them in a warehouse, models them into a clean orders table, and keeps that table updating hourly — so that a financial analyst can trust the number on tomorrow's revenue dashboard without ever touching a line of pipeline code.

02. The Data Engineering Lifecycle

Five stages, one of which never really stops running underneath the other four.

Reis and Housley frame the whole discipline around a single mental model: the data engineering lifecycle. Data is generated by some source system, ingested into environments you control, transformed into a usable shape, and served to whoever or whatever needs it next — a dashboard, a report, a model, an operational application. Storage is the fifth stage, but it behaves differently from the other four: rather than sitting at one point in the sequence, it underlies every stage at once. Raw events sit in storage before ingestion even starts; ingestion pipelines stage data in storage mid-flight; transformation jobs read from and write back to storage repeatedly; serving layers cache results in yet more storage. Treating storage as "the box between ingestion and transformation" badly undersells how central it is.

The lifecycle is a useful lens precisely because it forces a question at every stage: what does this data actually need right now, and what does the next stage need from me? A pipeline that ingests data perfectly but transforms it into a form nobody can query is a failure of the lifecycle just as much as one that never lands the data at all. Good data engineers think about all five stages together, because a decision made during ingestion (say, flattening a nested JSON payload) can quietly determine how expensive or awkward transformation becomes three stages later.

It's also worth being explicit about what the lifecycle is not: a one-way pipe. Real systems loop back on themselves constantly — transformed data gets re-ingested as a source for another pipeline, served outputs get stored again as inputs to a model, and quality issues discovered at the serving stage send engineers back to fix generation-side assumptions. The stages are a way of organizing the work, not a strict order of operations.

In practice the process is iterative and often cyclical: transformed data frequently gets re-ingested as a source for another pipeline, quality problems discovered at the serving stage send you back to fix transformation logic (or even generation-side assumptions), and "final" tables get reprocessed as business definitions change. Design your systems assuming data will flow back through storage and transformation more than once.

03. The Six Undercurrents

Concerns you never "finish" — they run underneath and across the whole lifecycle, continuously.

If the lifecycle (Section 02) describes the stages data moves through, the six undercurrents describe the concerns that never go away once a stage is "done." Security, data management, DataOps, data architecture, orchestration, and software engineering all apply to generation, storage, ingestion, transformation, and serving simultaneously — they're not boxes you check off once and move past.

This is where a lot of early-career data engineering effort goes wrong: it's easy to focus entirely on getting data from point A to point B and treat security, testing, documentation, and monitoring as things to bolt on "later, once the pipeline works." The undercurrents are the reason that approach breaks down as systems grow — a pipeline that works today but has no tests, no access controls, and no observability will eventually fail in a way nobody notices until a stakeholder complains.

Undercurrent

A cross-cutting concern that applies continuously across every stage of the data engineering lifecycle, rather than a stage you move through and complete. The metaphor: a river's current runs beneath the surface the entire length of the river, not just at one bend — undercurrents run beneath generation, storage, ingestion, transformation, and serving the entire time a system is alive.

UndercurrentOne-Line DefinitionRed Flag When Missing
SecurityLeast-privilege access and protection of data at every stage (Section 15)Everyone on the team has admin/production access "to move faster"
Data ManagementGovernance, quality, discoverability, and metadata (Section 14)Nobody can say confidently which table is the source of truth
DataOpsAutomation, testing, monitoring, and incident response for pipelines (Section 13)A broken pipeline is discovered by an angry stakeholder, not an alert

Software EngineeringVersion control, testing, code review, and CI/CD applied to pipeline codePipeline logic lives only in untested notebooks or one-off scripts

Why this matters more as you scale: at a two-person data team, weak undercurrents are annoying but survivable — everyone knows where the bodies are buried. As a team grows and the maturity model in Section 04 moves from "Starting with Data" toward "Leading with Data," the cost of ignoring undercurrents compounds: untested pipelines multiply, undocumented tables become landmines, and access sprawl becomes a genuine security incident waiting to happen. Mature data organizations are distinguished less by fancier tools and more by how seriously they treat these six concerns.

04. Data Maturity & the Data Engineer's Place on a Team

Where a team sits on the maturity curve shapes what a data engineer is actually asked to do.

Not every organization needs — or can support — the same kind of data engineering. Reis and Housley describe a three-stage data maturity model that maps roughly to how much an organization has invested in, and benefited from, its data capabilities. Recognizing which stage your own organization sits in is one of the more useful diagnostic exercises a data engineer can run, because the "right" architecture and the "right" undercurrent investment (Section 03) look very different at each stage.

Stage 1, Starting with Data, is ad hoc and reactive: reporting is manual, pipelines are fragile one-off scripts, and there's often no single person or team formally responsible for data. Stage 2, Scaling with Data, is where a formal data team and real tooling appear — but the team is still frequently firefighting, and technical debt accumulates almost as fast as new capability. Stage 3, Leading with Data, is where data becomes a genuine competitive advantage: automation is the default, self-service analytics actually works, and the organization's culture treats data-informed decisions as normal rather than exceptional.

Where the Data Engineer Sits

A data engineer typically reports into a Head of Data, a Chief Data Officer, or a VP of Engineering, depending on how the organization has decided to structure its data function. Day to day, though, the role is defined less by the reporting line and more by its collaborations: data scientists depend on the engineer for reliable, feature-ready data; analytics engineers depend on well-organized raw sources to model into marts; software engineers need the data engineer to respect production system boundaries when ingesting from operational databases; ML engineers need consistent, low-latency feature delivery; and business stakeholders need someone who can translate a vague ask ("can we see which customers are at risk of churning") into a concrete data product.

The book is direct about one failure mode here: the ivory-tower anti-pattern, where data engineers build technically impressive pipelines in isolation from the people who actually need the data, guided only by their own assumptions about what "good" looks like. The fix isn't more engineering — it's more conversation. Understanding what a stakeholder actually needs, and why, before writing a line of pipeline code is one of the highest-leverage habits a data engineer can build.

Anti-pattern to avoid: building "for the architecture" instead of for the business. A pipeline that is technically elegant but answers a question nobody asked is worse than a scrappy one that ships the number a stakeholder actually needs this week. Maturity (per the table above) is ultimately measured by outcomes, not by how sophisticated the stack looks.

05. Designing Good Data Architecture — Principles

Architecture as a series of trade-off decisions, not a picture you draw once and frame on the wall

In Reis and Housley's framing, data architecture is the design of systems that support an organization's current and evolving data needs — a synthesis of the business's requirements with the practical realities of the tools, teams, and time available to build with. It is not a fixed blueprint copied from a vendor's reference diagram, and it is not something a single senior architect hands down from on high. It is the accumulated result of many decisions, most of which should be made deliberately, evaluated against real trade-offs, and revisited as circumstances change.

Because almost every architecture decision trades one property for another — cost for latency, simplicity for flexibility, speed for durability — the book proposes nine principles as a shared vocabulary for making those trade-offs well. They are not a checklist to satisfy once; they are lenses to keep reapplying as the lifecycle (Section 02) and the undercurrents (Section 03) evolve underneath the architecture.

Loosely coupled systems

A system is loosely coupled when its components interact only through well-defined, stable interfaces — and know as little as possible about each other's internals. Changing, redeploying, or even replacing one component should not require a synchronized change to the others, as long as everyone still honors the shared contract.

Example: if the orders service and the analytics pipeline communicate only through a versioned event schema on a message bus, the orders team can rewrite their entire service internals next quarter and the analytics team never has to know, as long as the events keep the same shape.

The over-architecting trap

Principle 7 cuts both ways. It is just as expensive to build in flexibility or scale the business does not yet need as it is to lock yourself into a rigid system — both are decisions that are costly to unwind. A team that spends months building a multi-region, infinitely-scalable event mesh for a product with a few hundred daily users has made an "irreversible" investment of a different kind: sunk engineering time, and often a more complex system that is now harder to change than a plain, boring one would have been. Architect for the scale and flexibility you can reasonably foresee, not for a hypothetical future.

A concrete reversible decision

Storing raw and modeled data in an open table format with standard SQL as the primary interface (rather than a proprietary storage engine with its own export-limited format) is a textbook reversible decision. If a better or cheaper query engine appears in three years, the data can simply be pointed at it. Compare that to a proprietary format with no straightforward export path: switching later means a costly migration project, not a config change — see Section 06 for how open table formats put this into practice.

06. Major Architecture Patterns: Warehouse, Lake, Lakehouse & Mesh

Four recurring shapes for organizing data at scale, each with its own failure modes

Most real-world data platforms are variations on a small number of recurring architecture patterns. None of them is universally "correct" — each represents a different set of trade-offs among structure, governance, flexibility, and organizational scale, echoing the trade-off mindset from Section 05. Understanding where each pattern came from and where it tends to break down is more useful than memorizing a single "modern" answer.

The data warehouse: Inmon vs. Kimball

The data warehouse emerged to serve analytical (OLAP) workloads — aggregations and scans across large volumes of history — separately from the operational (OLTP) systems tuned for fast, small, transactional reads and writes. Two competing philosophies shaped how warehouses got built. Bill Inmon's top-down approach starts with a single normalized enterprise data warehouse modeling the whole business, from which downstream, denormalized data marts are later derived for specific teams. Ralph Kimball's bottom-up approach instead builds dimensional data marts first — fast, pragmatic, tied to specific business processes — and conforms their shared dimensions over time into a de facto enterprise view. In practice, most real warehouses borrow from both: some centrally modeled core tables, plus pragmatic marts layered on top.

The data lake and the "data swamp" risk

The data lake pattern stores raw data cheaply and at scale in object storage, deferring schema decisions to read time (schema-on-read) rather than enforcing structure at write time. This gives enormous flexibility — any data, any format, any volume — but that same flexibility is the pattern's biggest risk: without deliberate organization, a lake accumulates undocumented, untrustworthy files and becomes a data swamp. A widely used convention (not a rule from the book itself, but a common industry pattern) organizes lake storage into zones — often called raw/bronze (unmodified source data), cleaned/silver (validated, deduplicated, typed), and curated/gold (business-ready, aggregated) — to keep the lake navigable as it grows.

The data lakehouse

The lakehouse pattern layers open table formats — Apache Iceberg, Delta Lake, and Apache Hudi are the three dominant implementations — on top of plain object storage. These formats add transaction logs that provide ACID (atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability) guarantees, enforced (and evolvable) schemas, and time travel to prior table states, all features previously exclusive to warehouses. The practical payoff is that one copy of data, sitting in cheap object storage, can serve both BI/SQL workloads and ML/feature-engineering workloads, instead of duplicating data into a separate warehouse for one audience and a lake for the other (details on file and table formats in Section 09). Through 2025–2026 the industry trend has continued toward reducing lock-in between engines: Iceberg's REST catalog standard and Delta Lake's UniForm interoperability layer both aim to let multiple query engines read the same physical tables without conversion, pushing the ecosystem closer to genuinely engine-agnostic storage.

Data mesh

Data mesh, as articulated by Zhamak Dehghani, addresses an organizational bottleneck rather than a storage-technology problem: a single central data team struggling to serve an entire large organization. It rests on four principles: domain-oriented ownership (the teams closest to a data source own its data products), data as a product (data is published with the same rigor as a customer-facing product — documented, discoverable, with quality SLAs, or service-level agreements, defining what "reliable" means), self-serve data platform (central platform teams provide infrastructure so domains don't each reinvent pipelines), and federated computational governance (global standards enforced through automation, not a central approval queue). Mesh can run on top of a warehouse, a lake, or a lakehouse — it is a socio-technical operating model layered over whichever storage pattern an organization already has.

Lambda vs. Kappa: reconciling batch and real time

Two architectural answers exist for systems that must serve both historical and real-time views. Lambda architecture runs a batch layer and a separate speed (streaming) layer in parallel, later reconciling their outputs into a merged serving layer — powerful but operationally heavy, since the same logic often has to be implemented and kept consistent twice. Kappa architecture simplifies this by treating batch as a special case of streaming: a single streaming pipeline (with replay from a durable log) handles both real-time and historical processing, at the cost of requiring a stream-processing engine capable of efficient large-scale reprocessing.

07. Choosing Technologies Across the Lifecycle

A tool-agnostic framework for deciding what to adopt, and when to say no

The data engineering landscape reinvents its tooling every few years, which makes any specific product recommendation a poor long-term investment. What holds up instead is a repeatable framework for evaluating technology choices at every lifecycle stage (Section 02): a small set of questions that apply whether the decision is a database, an orchestrator, or a BI tool, and whether the year is 2022 or 2030.

The framework is deliberately not a scorecard that spits out one right answer — it is a way of surfacing trade-offs explicitly so a team makes them on purpose instead of by default. It also ties directly back to Section 05's reversibility principle: the technology decision itself should be evaluated for how expensive it will be to unwind later, not just how well it performs today.

Vendor lock-in via speed to market

Optimizing purely for speed to market often means accepting a vendor's proprietary formats, APIs, or query dialect to get moving fast. That is a legitimate trade-off if made consciously — but teams frequently make it by default, without weighing the migration cost against the time saved. A fast decision that is expensive to reverse violates the spirit of Section 05's reversibility principle even if it never appears on a risk register.

Favor boring technology

The "boring technology" principle: reserve genuinely novel, unproven technology for the small number of areas that are an actual competitive differentiator, and default to mature, well-understood, widely supported tools for everything else. A team has a limited budget of organizational attention and operational risk it can spend on the unfamiliar — spend it where it earns the business something distinctive, not on reinventing a message queue or a scheduler.

08. Data Generation in Source Systems

You don't design the source system — you inherit whatever schema, volume, and reliability it gives you.

Lifecycle stage: Generation

Every data pipeline starts somewhere it doesn't control. Before a single row reaches a warehouse or lakehouse, it was created by a source system — an application database recording a purchase, a mobile app emitting a tap event, a sensor reporting a temperature, a SaaS vendor exposing an API. Those systems were almost never designed with analytics in mind. They were designed by application engineers to do one job well: serve a product, process a transaction, keep a service running. Data engineers are, in the book's framing, consumers of someone else's system — and the first real skill of the job is evaluating that system honestly before deciding how to ingest from it.

That evaluation has to look past "what tables exist" and into how the system actually behaves. Is the schema fixed and enforced, or does it change without warning because a document store lets application engineers add fields at will? Is data mostly appended once and left alone, or are rows updated and deleted constantly, which changes what "capturing the data" even means? What volume and velocity does it produce on an ordinary day versus a peak day? What latency does the business genuinely need — not what sounds impressive — and what consistency guarantees does the source actually offer once you start reading from replicas instead of the primary?

The variety of source systems a data platform touches is wide: relational OLTP databases behind core applications, NoSQL stores behind newer or higher-throughput services, third-party APIs and SaaS platforms whose schemas you don't control at all, application and clickstream logs generated at high volume with little structure, IoT and sensor devices streaming continuously over unreliable networks, and flat files handed over by partners on whatever schedule suits them. Each shows up at the ingestion boundary (see Section 10) looking nothing like the others.

OLTP vs. OLAP

OLTP (Online Transaction Processing) systems are built to record many small transactions quickly and reliably — a core banking system posting one debit and one credit per transfer, over and over, correctly and fast. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) systems are built for the opposite workload: scanning and aggregating large volumes of historical data to answer broad questions.

Example — a core banking database is OLTP: it optimizes for "update this one account balance correctly, right now." A data warehouse built from years of that same ledger data is OLAP: it optimizes for "sum every transaction across every account for the last five years." Most of data engineering is the safe, repeatable movement of data from the OLTP world into the OLAP world.

09. Storage: Raw Ingredients, Files & Table Formats

Storage is not one thing — it's a stack of layers, and each layer adds a different capability.

Lifecycle stage: Storage

Storage sits underneath nearly every other stage of the data engineering lifecycle, which is why the book treats it as a foundation rather than a single technology choice. At the bottom is physical media — disk, SSD, memory — with real trade-offs in cost, speed, and durability. On top of that sits a file format, which decides how bytes are laid out on that media. On top of the file format sits a table format, a metadata layer that turns a pile of files into something that behaves like an actual database table. And on top of all of that sit the query engines that read and write through the whole stack.

Object storage has become the default base layer for analytical data over the last decade, and for good reason: it is durable (data is typically replicated across multiple zones), cheap relative to block storage, scales to enormous volumes without capacity planning, and — critically — decouples storage from compute, so you can scale the two independently instead of buying bigger servers just to hold more data.

Row-based vs. columnar storage

Row-based formats (CSV, JSON, Avro) store every field of one record together, which suits workloads that read or write a whole record at a time — exactly what an OLTP application does. Columnar formats (Parquet, ORC) store every value of one column together across all records, which suits analytical queries that touch only a handful of columns across a huge number of rows.

Example — a table has a billion rows and 40 columns, and an analyst asks for the average transaction amount by month. A row-based file forces a scan that reads all 40 columns for all billion rows just to reach the one column it needs. A columnar file lets the engine read only the amount and date columns, skipping the other 38 entirely — often an order-of-magnitude reduction in bytes read.

Table formats: metadata that turns files into a table

A file format alone doesn't give you a table — it gives you a folder full of Parquet files. A table format is the metadata layer on top that adds the things a database traditionally provided: ACID transactions (so a writer failing halfway through doesn't leave the table half-updated), schema enforcement and evolution (so adding a column doesn't break every existing reader), time travel (querying the table as it looked at a previous point), and safe concurrent access from multiple engines reading and writing the same underlying files.

Three open table formats dominate current practice, and the book's own preference is to stay format-neutral rather than pick a winner — the right choice depends on existing tooling and engine mix. Apache Iceberg originated at Netflix and is now developed as an Apache project with broad backing across many query engines; it's generally recognized for a well-specified, engine-agnostic metadata layer and mature support for large-scale schema and partition evolution. Delta Lake originated at Databricks and is open-sourced under the Linux Foundation; it's generally recognized for its tight integration with Spark-based pipelines and its mature transaction log. Apache Hudi originated at Uber and is also an Apache project; it's generally recognized for strong support of incremental upserts and streaming or CDC-style ingestion directly into the lake.

Through 2025 and into 2026, the practical cost of choosing among the three has been dropping: REST-based catalogs and cross-format conversion layers increasingly let a table written in one format be read by engines built around another, reducing the lock-in risk that made this choice feel so consequential a few years earlier. Sitting alongside all of this is the data catalog — the metadata layer that makes stored tables discoverable, documented, and governed, covered in more depth in Section 14.

Why time travel matters — because table formats keep a versioned history of every commit to a table, you can query it exactly as it looked at a past point in time. That matters far beyond curiosity: when a downstream number looks wrong, time travel lets you compare today's table state against yesterday's to isolate exactly what changed, and auditors or regulators can be shown precisely what a table contained on a given date — reproducibility that a plain folder of files can't offer.

10. Ingestion: Batch, Streaming, CDC & ETL vs ELT

The real question isn't "batch or streaming" — it's "how fast does this actually need to be?"

Lifecycle stage: Ingestion

Ingestion is the stage that physically moves data across the boundary from a source system (Section 08) into storage (Section 09) that the data platform controls. The most visible design choice is batch versus streaming: batch ingestion collects bounded chunks of data — an hour's worth, a day's worth — and moves them on a schedule; streaming ingestion treats data as an unbounded, continuous flow and moves each event (or small micro-batch of events) with much lower latency. Batch is simpler to build, cheaper to run, and easier to debug; streaming is more operationally complex — more moving parts, more ways to fail — but delivers data with far less delay. The book's advice is blunt: don't default to streaming because it sounds more impressive. Most analytical use cases tolerate minutes or hours of latency just fine, and the added complexity of streaming should be justified by an actual business requirement.

Ingestion also has a direction: push systems have the source actively send data to you (a webhook firing on an event, an application publishing to a queue), while pull systems have the ingestion layer reach out and request data on its own schedule (an API poller, a scheduled query against a source database). Most real pipelines use a mix, depending on what each source system supports.

Before picking any specific tool, the book frames ingestion as a set of questions worth answering explicitly: is the data bounded or unbounded? What frequency does the business genuinely require, versus what sounds impressive? Is the interaction synchronous or asynchronous? What serialization format is the data in, and does it need to change along the way? What throughput and scalability does the pipeline need to sustain, including at peak? And what reliability and durability guarantees exist if a network call fails or a consumer crashes mid-read?

Change Data Capture

Change Data Capture (CDC)

CDC is a family of techniques for capturing only the rows that changed in a source system — inserts, updates, and deletes — instead of repeatedly copying an entire table. It turns "what does this table look like right now?" into a continuous stream of "what just changed?", which is both cheaper and far more timely for large, frequently updated tables.

Example — instead of re-exporting a 200-million-row accounts table every night just to catch the handful of accounts that changed, a CDC connector emits a small, continuous stream of only those changed rows, which downstream systems apply as updates.

There are three common ways to implement CDC, and they differ mainly in where they get their signal from. Log-based CDC reads a database's own transaction (write-ahead) log — the same log the database uses internally for replication and crash recovery — which makes it the lowest-overhead and most reliable approach, since it doesn't touch live tables at all; tools like Debezium work this way. Trigger-based CDC attaches database triggers to fire on every insert, update, and delete, writing the change to a separate change table; it's straightforward to reason about but adds real overhead to every write on the source database. Diff or query-based CDC periodically queries a table filtered by an updated_at or version column; it's the simplest to build with no special database features required, but it can miss deletes entirely (a deleted row leaves nothing to query for) and adds recurring load from repeated polling.

Message queues and the publish/subscribe pattern

Streaming ingestion is usually backed by a message queue or event-streaming platform — Kafka, Kinesis, Pub/Sub-style systems, described generically here since the book stays tool-neutral. These systems implement a publish/subscribe pattern: producers publish events to a named topic without knowing who will read them, and consumers subscribe to the topics they care about, each processing events independently and at their own pace. This decoupling is what lets one stream of events feed several different downstream systems — a real-time fraud check, an analytics pipeline, and an archival job — without the producer knowing any of them exist.

ETL, ELT, and EtLT

The other major ingestion-adjacent decision is where transformation happens relative to loading. In ETL (Extract, Transform, Load), data is transformed in a separate processing layer before it ever lands in the target system — common when storage was expensive and warehouses had limited compute. In ELT (Extract, Load, Transform), raw data is loaded first and transformed afterward, inside the warehouse or lakehouse itself, using its own elastic compute. ELT became the dominant pattern as cloud object storage made holding raw data cheap and cloud warehouses made on-demand transformation compute abundant — there was no longer a strong reason to transform before you could afford to store the raw version. EtLT is a hybrid: a light transformation happens before load — commonly PII (personally identifiable information) masking, deduplication, or basic type conformance — with the heavier business-logic transformation still happening after load, closer to where analysts and modeling tools (Section 11) work.

The trigger-based CDC trap — trigger-based CDC is tempting because it needs no special database configuration, but every insert, update, and delete on the source table now also pays the cost of firing a trigger and writing to a change table. On a busy production OLTP database — a core banking system processing thousands of transactions a second, for instance — that overhead compounds, and it's not unusual for trigger-based CDC to visibly slow down the very application it was meant to observe. Log-based CDC avoids this because it reads a log the database already maintains for its own purposes.

Idempotent ingestion — at-least-once delivery means a message might be delivered more than once after a retry; exactly-once delivery means it's delivered precisely once, but is much harder to guarantee end-to-end and often comes with a performance cost. Most real pipelines build on at-least-once delivery and make the ingestion step idempotent instead — writing changes so that applying the same event twice produces the same result as applying it once (for example, an upsert keyed on a stable event ID, rather than a blind append). That way, when a pipeline retries after a crash or a network failure, it doesn't silently double-count data.

11. Queries, Modeling & Transformation

Shaping data so it can be queried fast, and so five people who ask the same question get the same answer.

Once data has landed in a warehouse or lakehouse (see Section 10), two questions follow immediately: how do we query it efficiently, and how do we structure it so that "revenue last quarter" means the same thing to every analyst who asks? The first question is about query performance; the second is about data modeling. Neither is glamorous, but together they determine whether a data platform is fast, trustworthy, and maintainable — or a slow, contradictory mess that people quietly route around with their own spreadsheets.

Query optimization is worth a light pass. Modern query engines lean on predicate pushdown (filtering rows as close to storage as possible, before they are pulled into memory), partition pruning (skipping entire files or partitions that cannot match a filter, e.g. querying WHERE order_date = '2026-07-01' against data partitioned by date), and column pruning in columnar formats (reading only the columns a query actually references instead of full rows). Reading an execution plan — even at a glance — tells you whether the engine is doing a targeted scan or a brute-force full-table scan, which is usually the single biggest lever on cost and latency. But performance tuning is secondary to a harder problem: deciding how tables relate to each other in the first place. That is what data modeling solves, and the field has produced several durable, competing answers.

From Normalization to Dimensional Modeling

Operational (OLTP) systems normalize their schemas — organizing tables so each fact lives in exactly one place, formalized through 1NF (atomic column values, no repeating groups), 2NF (no attribute depends on only part of a composite key), and 3NF (no attribute depends on a non-key attribute). Normalization exists to prevent update anomalies: if a customer's address is stored in twenty rows, an update has to hit all twenty consistently or the data drifts. That discipline is exactly right for a system processing thousands of small transactional writes per second. It is often exactly wrong for a system whose job is to answer analytical questions over billions of rows, because every normalized join adds cost. Analytical modeling therefore branched into several distinct schools of thought, each still actively used today.

Star Schema

The signature output of dimensional modeling: a central fact table holding numeric measurements or events at a defined grain — grain means what one row represents, e.g. one row per line item rather than one row per order — surrounded by dimension tables holding the descriptive context needed to slice and filter those facts. The fact table is typically long and narrow with foreign keys plus measures; dimensions are wide and relatively short, full of readable attributes analysts filter and group by.

Example: a fact_sales table with one row per line item (date key, product key, store key, customer key, quantity, revenue) joined to dim_date, dim_product, dim_store, and dim_customer — so "revenue by product category last month" is a single join against dim_product, instead of chasing the same answer through several normalized source tables.

Two mechanics come up constantly in dimensional models. Slowly Changing Dimensions (SCD) handle the fact that descriptive attributes change over time: SCD Type 1 simply overwrites the old value (you lose history, but the model stays simple — fine for correcting a typo in a customer's name); SCD Type 2 inserts a new row with new effective-date bounds and keeps the old row intact (you preserve history — necessary when "what did this customer's tier look like at the time of the order" actually matters). Data Vault, by contrast, separates the stable business key (hub) from its relationships (links) and from every version of its descriptive attributes (satellites), which makes it resilient to upstream schema churn and easy to audit, at the cost of needing a Kimball- or OBT-style layer on top before it's pleasant for analysts to query directly.

Batch, Streaming, and the Semantic Layer

The "T" in ELT is most often executed today as SQL-based batch transformation: a tool reads raw or lightly-modeled tables, applies a chain of SQL models, runs automated tests against the results, and generates a lineage graph — a map of which tables and columns a given dataset was derived from, and everything downstream that depends on it — a pattern popularized by dbt-style tooling but not unique to any single product. Streaming transformation solves a related but distinct problem: computing aggregates over data that never stops arriving, using windowing — grouping continuous events into fixed chunks for aggregation, e.g. non-overlapping "tumbling" windows, overlapping "sliding" windows, or activity-bounded "session" windows — and watermarks (a heuristic for how long to wait for late-arriving events before considering a window "closed") — see Section 10 for the ingestion side of that story.

A newer layer sits above both: the semantic (metrics) layer, a pattern seen in dbt's Semantic Layer, LookML, and Cube. It defines a metric like "active customer" or "net revenue" exactly once — including its filters, joins, and time grain — and lets every downstream BI tool query that single definition instead of re-implementing it in SQL five separate times.

The five-dashboards trap. Without a shared metric definition, "monthly active users" ends up computed slightly differently in five dashboards — one excludes trial accounts, one uses UTC and another local time, one counts logins and another counts any event. Every number is individually defensible and collectively useless the moment two of them appear in the same meeting. A semantic layer doesn't add a new feature so much as it removes five silent, competing implementations of the same idea.

12. Serving Data: Analytics, ML & Reverse ETL

The last-mile stage of the lifecycle — where curated data finally becomes someone's dashboard, model, or CRM field.

Serving is the payoff stage of the data engineering lifecycle: everything upstream — generation, storage, ingestion, transformation — exists to produce data that is trustworthy enough to hand to a human decision-maker, a customer-facing product, or a machine learning model. It is also the stage where requirements diverge most sharply, because "serving data" covers everything from a monthly board report to a fraud check that must resolve in milliseconds. Getting the architecture right means matching each consumer to a serving pattern with the right latency, freshness, and access characteristics — not defaulting every use case to "put it in the warehouse and let people query it."

The most familiar pattern is BI and self-service analytics: dashboards and ad hoc query tools sitting on top of curated marts or an OBT layer, used by analysts and business stakeholders for retrospective and strategic questions. Embedded analytics takes the same underlying data and surfaces it inside a customer-facing product itself — a usage-statistics panel inside a SaaS app, for instance — which raises different concerns around multi-tenant isolation and query cost per end user. Operational analytics is a third, easily confused category: near-real-time dashboards used to run the business minute-to-minute (queue depth, current fraud-alert volume, live inventory) rather than to reflect on last quarter.

ML serving, from a data engineering vantage point, mostly comes down to one responsibility: producing feature data that is reliable, correctly modeled, and consistent between training and serving. What happens after that splits into two very different worlds. Batch inference scores a model against a stored dataset on a schedule (e.g., nightly churn scores for every customer) and tolerates the same latency as any other batch job. Online inference serves a prediction synchronously against a live request — a fraud check during checkout, a recommendation on page load — and demands single-digit-to-double-digit millisecond response times, which usually means a dedicated low-latency feature store or cache rather than a query against the warehouse itself.

Reverse ETL

The practice of syncing curated, computed data out of the warehouse or lakehouse and back into the operational SaaS tools that business teams already live in — inverting the usual ELT direction of flow. It exists because the warehouse is a great place to compute an insight but a terrible place for a salesperson or support agent to go looking for it.

Example: a churn-risk model runs in the warehouse, and a reverse ETL job pushes the resulting score into a custom field on each account record in the CRM — so a salesperson sees "churn risk: high" right next to the account they're already working, without ever opening a BI tool.

13. Orchestration & DataOps

The scheduling backbone and the operational discipline that keep a multi-stage pipeline from silently rotting.

Orchestration is the practice of coordinating dependent tasks across the data engineering lifecycle — triggering an extract only after a source is ready, running a transform only after every input table has loaded, and notifying someone the moment any of that breaks. The standard mental model is a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG): nodes are tasks, edges are dependencies, "directed" means each edge has a direction (task A must finish before task B starts), and "acyclic" means no chain of dependencies ever loops back on itself — a task can never transitively depend on its own output.

It's tempting to reach for cron instead of a dedicated orchestrator, and for a single isolated script, that's fine. The trouble starts the moment a pipeline has more than one step. Cron can trigger a script at a fixed time, full stop — it has no native concept of "wait until the upstream job actually succeeded," no built-in retry-with-backoff, no structured alerting when a run fails, and no visual record of which jobs depend on which. Dedicated orchestrators — illustrative examples include Airflow, Dagster, and Prefect — exist specifically to express those dependencies as data (the DAG itself), handle retries and backfills, and make the whole dependency graph inspectable instead of tribal knowledge scattered across shell scripts.

DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph)

A graph where every edge points in one direction and following any chain of edges can never lead back to the starting node. In orchestration, nodes represent tasks and edges represent "must finish before" dependencies; the acyclic property guarantees the scheduler can always find a valid order to run every task.

Example: "extract orders" and "extract customers" must both finish before "join & transform" can start; "join & transform" must finish before "load to warehouse"; nothing in that chain ever points back to "extract orders," so the graph is valid and schedulable.

"Cron is not an orchestrator." Teams that lean on cron alone for anything beyond a single standalone script tend to discover the gap the hard way: a job fails silently at 3 a.m. with no retry and no alert, a downstream script starts reading a table before the upstream job finished writing it, and the only documentation of "script B depends on script A" is a comment someone wrote two years ago and never updated. Software Engineering: the sixth undercurrent, in its own right

DataOps' automation pillar covers how pipeline changes ship; the software engineering undercurrent from Section 03 is the broader discipline underneath that — treating pipeline code with the same rigor as any other production codebase. In practice that means: version control for every transformation and pipeline definition, not just ad hoc notebooks; automated tests at multiple levels (unit tests for individual transformation logic, integration tests for a full pipeline run, data tests for the output's shape and values); code review before a change reaches production; and infrastructure as code — defining warehouses, clusters, and permissions in versioned configuration files instead of clicking through a console, so an environment can be recreated, audited, or torn down exactly the same way twice. The book frames this as a genuine engineering discipline a data team has to build deliberately, not a byproduct that shows up automatically once a team adopts an orchestrator.

14. Data Governance, Quality & Master Data Management

Discoverability, quality, and accountability — the practices that keep a growing data estate trustworthy

Everything covered from Section 08 through Section 13 assumes someone can find the data, understands what it means, and trusts the numbers in it. That trust does not appear on its own. As an organization's data footprint grows past a handful of tables, informal tribal knowledge — "ask the person who built it" — stops scaling, and the absence of formal governance starts showing up as duplicate metrics, silent breakage, and datasets nobody wants to own. Reis and Housley frame governance around three practical pillars: discoverability, security, and accountability — with data quality treated as a constantly-monitored companion to all three, and covered in its own depth below.

Discoverability asks whether people can find out that a dataset exists at all, and understand what it represents without interrogating its creator. This is the job of a data catalog and its underlying metadata — schema definitions, ownership, freshness, lineage, and business-friendly descriptions that translate a column name like acct_stat_cd into "account status code." Good metadata management turns tribal knowledge into something searchable, and it is a precondition for almost everything else in this section: you cannot govern, secure, or assess the quality of a dataset that governance tooling does not know exists.

Data quality asks a narrower question about a dataset that has already been found: is it accurate, complete, and fit for the purpose someone is about to use it for? Quality is not a single yes/no property — it is usually broken into distinct dimensions, each of which fails in its own characteristic way. Accountability, the third pillar, asks who is responsible when quality slips or a definition changes. Without a named steward, a data quality incident becomes everyone's problem and therefore no one's problem.

The dimensions of data quality

DimensionWhat it meansExample failure
AccuracyValues correctly reflect the real-world fact they represent.A customer's recorded date of birth is off by one day due to a timezone conversion bug.
CompletenessAll the records and fields that should be present are present.A batch load silently drops the last partition of the day because a job timed out.
ConsistencyThe same fact agrees across tables, systems, or time.A customer's account status is "active" in core banking but "closed" in the CRM extract.
TimelinessData is available within the window a consumer needs it.A risk dashboard refreshes on yesterday's transactions, but the desk needs same-day exposure.

ValidityValues conform to an expected format, range, or domain.An interest rate field contains a negative value that passed no schema constraint.

Because none of these dimensions is checked "once and done," modern practice increasingly favors data observability: automated, continuous monitoring of a pipeline's freshness, volume, schema, and value distributions, so an anomaly triggers an alert before a stakeholder notices a wrong number on a report. The industry shorthand for the failure mode this prevents is data downtime — the period a dataset is missing, wrong, or otherwise unusable, and nobody yet knows it.

Master Data Management and the golden record

Large organizations rarely have one system of record for a core entity like "customer," "account," or "product." Core banking, CRM, card processing, and digital channels each accumulate their own version of the same customer, often with different spellings, different identifiers, and different update timestamps. Master Data Management (MDM) is the discipline of reconciling those competing records into a single, trusted representation — matching records that likely refer to the same entity, resolving conflicts between them, and publishing one authoritative version for the rest of the organization to consume.

Golden record

The single, reconciled version of a real-world entity's data that an organization treats as authoritative, produced by matching and merging records from multiple source systems.

Example: Core banking spells a customer's name "NGUYEN VAN A" with an old mailing address; the CRM has the correct current address but a slightly different name format; the card system has a verified phone number the other two lack. MDM matches these three records as the same person and produces one golden record with the correct name, current address, and verified phone number — which downstream reporting, marketing, and risk systems then reference instead of picking a source at random.

For a bank, this is not an abstract data-modeling exercise. A single, accurate view of a customer across core banking, cards, and digital channels is an operational necessity — it underpins credit decisions, fraud checks, and service quality — and it is also the kind of control that regulators and internal risk or compliance functions generally expect to see maintained accurately and kept demonstrably reconciled, rather than assembled ad hoc when someone asks for it. As elsewhere in this guide, this is a generic illustration of why MDM matters for a bank, not a claim about any specific institution's actual controls.

15. Security, Privacy & Data Ethics

Least privilege, layered controls, and the difference between compliant and actually secure

Security is one of the six undercurrents from Section 03 precisely because it cannot be bolted onto a finished pipeline — it has to shape every decision from schema design to who gets a warehouse login. The book anchors its treatment of security in two foundational principles. Least privilege means every person, service account, and pipeline job gets the minimum access required to do its job, nothing more, for no longer than necessary. Defense in depth means no single control is trusted to hold on its own; if one layer fails, another layer should still catch the problem.

On top of those principles sit concrete technical controls. Encryption at rest and in transit protects data from anyone who gains access to storage media or network traffic without the right keys. Row-level and column-level security let a single table serve different audiences different slices of the same data — a loan officer sees their branch's applications, a regional manager sees the region, and a salary column stays hidden from everyone except HR and payroll. Access control models formalize who gets what: role-based access control (RBAC) grants permissions by job role, which scales well and is easy to audit, while attribute-based access control (ABAC) grants permissions dynamically based on attributes of the user, resource, and context — useful when access rules are too fine-grained for a fixed set of roles. Finally, masking and tokenization replace sensitive values with realistic-looking substitutes or opaque tokens so that most systems and most people never need to see the real value at all.

Anonymization vs. pseudonymization

Anonymization irreversibly strips or transforms identifying information so the record can no longer be linked back to a specific individual, by anyone, under any circumstances — done properly, it takes the data outside the scope of most personal-data rules. Pseudonymization replaces identifying fields with a token or key, but the original identity remains recoverable by whoever holds the separate mapping — it reduces exposure but does not remove the data from the scope of personal-data protection, because re-identification is still possible in principle.

Example: Replacing a customer ID with a random token stored in a separate lookup table is pseudonymization — someone with access to that lookup table can still identify the customer. Aggregating transaction data into "average spend per age band per province" with no way to trace a row back to an individual is closer to true anonymization.

Defense in depth, layer by layer

Regulatory landscape and privacy by design

Globally, GDPR (EU) and CCPA (California) are the best-known reference points for personal data protection principles: informed consent, purpose limitation (collect data for a stated reason and don't quietly reuse it for something else), a set of data subject rights (access, correction, deletion), and breach notification obligations. These frameworks have shaped how most data platforms think about personal data handling even outside their direct jurisdiction, because global vendors build to their baseline.

Vietnam's own personal data protection legal framework (PDPL) has been developing along a broadly similar direction — generally requiring lawful grounds and a degree of transparency for processing personal data, and giving individuals certain rights over their own data. For banks specifically, the State Bank of Vietnam's supervisory expectations commonly extend beyond privacy law generally into more specific banking-sector concerns: customer information confidentiality, careful handling of cross-border data transfers, and data localization considerations for certain categories of banking data. Because the specific legal instruments and their requirements evolve, a data engineering team should treat this section as a conceptual map, not a compliance checklist — the current, authoritative text should always come from legal and compliance colleagues, not from a summary of a 2022 book.

Beyond compliance, the book raises data ethics as a distinct concern: pipelines that feed machine learning models can encode and amplify bias present in historical data, producing unfair outcomes at scale even when no individual step was intentionally discriminatory. Privacy-enhancing techniques like k-anonymity (ensuring any individual record is indistinguishable from at least k−1 others on key attributes) and differential privacy (adding calibrated statistical noise so aggregate results are useful but no individual record can be reliably inferred) offer conceptual tools for reducing this risk. The umbrella idea the book advocates is privacy by design: treating privacy protection as an architectural requirement decided at design time, rather than a control retrofitted onto a pipeline after a stakeholder asks about it.

The book warns explicitly against security theater: a program that satisfies every item on a compliance checklist — encryption enabled, RBAC documented, an audit trail somewhere — while genuine risk remains unaddressed because the controls were designed to pass an audit rather than to stop a realistic attacker. Checklists are useful evidence of diligence, but they are not a substitute for asking "what would actually happen if this specific control failed," and a checkbox-driven program can create dangerous false confidence in exactly the moment it is tested.

16. The Future of Data Engineering

Where the field is heading, and why the fundamentals in this guide should outlast the current tool cycle

Reis and Housley end their book with deliberately modest predictions, and the years since publication have largely vindicated that caution: the specific product names have shifted, but the directional trends they and the surrounding industry pointed to have mostly held up. What follows blends the book's own forward-looking themes with developments that have become clear through 2025 and into 2026.

The most visible shift is a partial retreat from the "modern data stack" era's tool sprawl — a period in which a mid-size team might have run a dozen best-of-breed point solutions stitched together with custom glue code. Maintaining that many integration points turned out to be its own operational burden, and many teams are now consolidating onto fewer, more tightly integrated platforms, even at some cost to per-function best-in-class capability. Related to this is the continued rise of managed and serverless infrastructure: warehouses, streaming platforms, and orchestrators that used to require dedicated operational staff increasingly run as a managed service, shifting a data engineer's time away from keeping infrastructure alive and toward modeling, governance, and stakeholder-facing work — the higher-leverage activities the book argues engineers should be doing anyway.

Streaming is finishing its transition from specialized exception to default architectural option, as the operational maturity discussed in Section 10 continues to improve; teams increasingly reach for a streaming-capable pattern by default and fall back to batch only when latency truly does not matter. At the same time, data platforms are undergoing what might be called "enterprise-ization": governance, data quality monitoring, and FinOps — treating cloud cost as an accountable, forecastable line item rather than a surprise on the monthly bill — are maturing from afterthoughts bolted on late into standard practice built in from the start. Table format interoperability efforts between the Iceberg and Delta Lake ecosystems (see Section 09) are also lowering the cost of a wrong or outgrown table-format bet, softening one of the more consequential architecture decisions covered in Section 06.

Two more shifts deserve particular attention. First, the boundaries between data engineering, platform engineering, and ML engineering are blurring, as pipelines increasingly need to serve both BI dashboards and production ML features from shared infrastructure. Second, generative AI and large language models are becoming tools data engineers use, not only technologies they support: text-to-SQL interfaces extend self-service analytics to less technical stakeholders, LLM-assisted code generation speeds up writing and reviewing pipeline transformations, and automated documentation and lineage generation reduce the metadata burden discussed in Section 14. None of this replaces the engineering judgment this guide has covered — deciding what to build, what trade-off to accept, and what "correct" means for a given dataset remains a human responsibility that a code-generating assistant does not resolve on its own.

Data contracts

One structural response to the "silent upstream breakage" problem raised throughout this guide is the rise of data contracts: formal, versioned agreements between a data-producing team and its downstream consumers that specify a dataset's schema, semantics, and change-management process. A data contract turns an implicit, undocumented expectation — "please don't rename that column" — into an explicit, testable one, often enforced automatically in CI so that a breaking schema change fails a build rather than a downstream dashboard.

A data engineer who internalizes those principles can evaluate the next tool cycle — whatever it turns out to be — on its merits, instead of relearning data engineering from scratch every time the vendor landscape reshuffles.

17. Big Data & the Cloud

What actually makes data "big", and why elastic infrastructure changed the job

"Big data" and "the cloud" showed up in the same decade for a reason that is easy to miss once you are used to both: the moment a dataset outgrows a single machine, renting elastic, on-demand infrastructure becomes far more practical than buying and running your own data center. Nearly every architecture pattern in Section 06 and every technology-choice trade-off in Section 07 assumes this backdrop — so it is worth making explicit rather than leaving it as folklore.

The phrase "big data" has been diluted by marketing to the point of near-uselessness, but the underlying idea is precise: data earns the label when it stresses at least one dimension past what a single traditional system — one relational database on one server — can comfortably handle. The classic framing is the five V's.

DimensionWhat it meansWhen it becomes the constraint
VolumeThe sheer amount of data — terabytes to petabytes and beyond.The dataset no longer fits, or no longer processes in acceptable time, on one machine's disk and memory.
VelocityHow fast data arrives and needs to be processed — real-time streams, not just nightly batches.Events arrive faster than a batch window can absorb them, forcing the streaming approaches in Section 10.
VarietyStructured, semi-structured, and unstructured data all mixed together.A fixed relational schema can no longer describe every source, pushing you toward lake-style storage (Section 09).
VeracityHow trustworthy and accurate the data actually is once it arrives.Volume amplifies bad data — quality and governance (Section 14) stop being optional.
ValueWhether all that volume, velocity, and variety actually translates into a usable business outcome.Always. Data with no path to value is cost, not an asset — the first question, not the last.

"Big" is relative — and shrinking

What counted as "big data" requiring a cluster in 2012 often fits comfortably in a single modern cloud warehouse or even on one large machine today. The useful question is never "is this big data?" in the abstract — it is "does this dataset, at our velocity, still fit the simplest tool we already run?" Reach for distributed systems when the data forces you to, not because the term sounds impressive.

Why cloud computing changed data engineering

Before the cloud, handling big data meant buying physical servers years in advance, guessing at future capacity, and running your own data centers — capacity planning as a permanent, high-stakes bet. Cloud computing replaced that with elastic, pay-as-you-go infrastructure rented from a provider: compute and storage that scale up in minutes and scale back down just as fast. For data engineering specifically, that turned "provision a cluster" from a procurement project into an API call, and turned the separation of storage and compute (Section 09) from a theory into the default.

Providers package that infrastructure at three levels of abstraction — the difference is simply how much the provider manages for you.

ModelWhat the provider runsWhat you still own
IaaS — Infrastructure as a ServiceRaw virtual machines, storage, and networking.The operating system and everything above it — you patch, configure, and operate.
PaaS — Platform as a ServiceA managed runtime: a managed database, a managed Spark cluster, a managed orchestrator.Configuration, scheduling, and the pipelines themselves — but not the OS or patching.
SaaS — Software as a ServiceA complete, ready-to-use application.Nothing operationally — you log in and use it.

Where data engineering lives

Most modern data platforms — cloud data warehouses, managed Spark, managed orchestrators — are PaaS. You do not manage servers, but you very much configure, schedule, and monitor how they process your data. That PaaS layer is exactly where a data engineer operates day to day: high enough to not babysit hardware, low enough to control cost, performance, and correctness.

Very few organizations pick a cloud purely on technical merit — existing vendor relationships, compliance and data-residency requirements (for a Vietnamese bank, SBV expectations and PDPL obligations are often the deciding factor, not benchmarks), and team familiarity usually matter more. The good news: the data engineering concepts in this guide transfer directly regardless of whose product names you happen to be learning today. Confirm current regulatory obligations with legal/compliance before committing to a provider or region.

19. The Modern Data Stack

One tool per category, mapped onto the lifecycle — not one tool for everything

Instead of one monolithic platform, modern pipelines are usually assembled from specialized, best-of-breed tools — one per lifecycle stage — that interoperate through open storage and shared catalogs. This is the "modern data stack": less a fixed product list than a way of composing the lifecycle from Section 02 out of interchangeable parts. The categories map directly onto the lifecycle stages, which is exactly why the mental model in this guide keeps paying off no matter which vendors your team has standardized on.

Tool names change — categories don't

Specific vendors rise and fall, but the six categories above — sources, ingestion, storage, transformation, orchestration, serving — have stayed stable for over a decade. Learn each category's job first, then pick up whatever tool your team actually runs. This is the same durable-vs-transitory-primitives idea from Section 07, applied to the stack as a whole.

20. Skills & Career Path

Software-engineering rigor plus data-specific depth — and how the role grows

Data engineering blends software-engineering rigor with data-specific knowledge. No single skill defines the role; it is the combination — and the discipline to apply engineering practices (Section 13's DataOps) to data work — that separates a durable data platform from a pile of brittle scripts. The core skills below show up in almost every data engineering job, whatever the tool stack.

SkillWhy it matters
SQLThe single most-used skill — querying, windowing, and modeling data (Section 11). It is the lingua franca of the warehouse and rarely goes out of style.
Python (or a similar language)Scripting pipelines, calling APIs, writing transformation and glue logic that SQL alone cannot express.
Distributed-systems basicsUnderstanding how work is parallelized across machines and where bottlenecks and failures appear — the foundation under Spark, streaming, and the cloud.
Cloud platformsProvisioning and operating storage, compute, and networking in a cloud environment (Section 18).
Data modelingDesigning schemas that are both efficient to compute and intuitive to query (Section 11).
Software-engineering practicesVersion control, testing, code review, and CI/CD applied to pipelines — the difference between "it ran on my laptop" and "it runs every night, unattended, for years."

A typical growth path

Titles vary by company, but the arc of responsibility is consistent: you start by keeping existing pipelines healthy, grow into designing them, and eventually shape the architecture and the platform others build on.

  1. Junior Data Engineer — builds and maintains existing pipelines, writes SQL transformations, learns the stack.
  2. Data Engineer — designs new pipelines end-to-end and owns data quality for a domain.
  3. Senior / Staff Data Engineer — sets architecture direction, mentors, and solves cross-team platform problems.
  4. Data Platform Engineer / Data Architect — owns the overall data architecture strategy across the organization.

The through-line

Notice that every rung leans harder on the undercurrents from Section 03 — architecture, DataOps, security, governance — and less on any one tool. Careers in this field compound on judgment about trade-offs (Section 07), not on memorizing the current market leader's product names.

∎ The takeaway

Data engineering is not the plumbing you tolerate on the way to "the real work" of analytics or machine learning — it is the discipline that decides whether the real work is even trustworthy. The lifecycle (generation, storage, ingestion, transformation, serving) gives you a map of what has to happen to data. The undercurrents (security, data management, DataOps, architecture, orchestration, software engineering) are what separate a pipeline a team can sleep soundly on from one that pages someone every night. And the recurring theme across every chapter of the source book — architecture, tool choice, modeling, even ethics — is that data engineering is a series of trade-offs made under real constraints, not a search for one universally correct answer.

If you take one habit from this guide into daily work: before building anything, ask what stage of the lifecycle you are in, which undercurrents you are about to neglect, and whether the decision you are making is reversible. Most expensive data engineering mistakes are the ones nobody asked those three questions about.
