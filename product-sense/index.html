Product Sense for Senior Engineers

Product sense is the round that increasingly decides senior offers — and it is no longer only a PM interview. It asks whether you can take a deliberately vague problem and work out who it is for, what actually hurts them, and what you would ship first. This is the whole round: the rubric they score you against, the 35-minute structure, the metrics and guardrails, the metric-drop playbook, the new AI product sense interview, and what changes if you are a data or AI engineer.

01. What "product sense" actually is — and why senior engineers now get this round

Product sense is the ability to look at a vague, under-specified problem and work out who it is for, what actually hurts them, and what the smallest useful thing you could ship is — out loud, under time pressure, without being handed a spec. It is not design talent. It is not having opinions about fonts. It is judgment about which problem deserves the engineering.

For a decade this was a product manager's interview. It has moved. The reason is blunt: the cost of building collapsed, so the value of choosing what to build went up. When a senior engineer can scaffold a working feature in an afternoon, the expensive mistake is no longer a slow implementation — it is six weeks spent shipping the wrong thing beautifully. Companies started screening for the failure mode that now costs them the most.

The one sentence that explains the whole round

They are not trying to find out whether your idea is good. They are trying to find out how you decide — because on the job, nobody will hand you a well-formed ticket, and they need to know what you do with the ambiguity. That is why a "boring" answer with a crisp, defended chain of reasoning beats a dazzling idea you cannot justify. Say the reasoning out loud. The reasoning is the deliverable.

That is the single biggest change in senior engineering interviews in the last two years.

03. The five things you are actually scored on

Published rubrics differ in wording but converge hard on five dimensions. The important structural fact: they do not average. You need to clear the bar on each one — a superb solution does not rescue a mushy segment.

1
Communication. Can they follow you without doing work? Do you signpost — "I'm going to do three things: pick a user, find their biggest pain, then design for it"?
2
Product motivation. Do you know why the product exists, who it beats, and what the company is actually trying to win? Or are you designing in a vacuum?
3
Segmentation. Did you choose one user and defend the choice? "Everyone who commutes" is not a segment.
4
Problem identification. Is the pain a real obstacle, specific, and prioritised against the others?
5
Solution & V1. Is it concrete enough to build, and does it solve the pain you just prioritised — not a different, more interesting one?

04. The five question types

Every prompt you will get is one of five. The good news: they all sit on the same spine. Only the entry point changes.

"Tell me about a product you love" is not small talk

It is the whole interview with the hard part hidden, and it is where the most candidates quietly fail — because they describe a product they enjoy instead of analysing it. The trap is that it feels like a chat. Answer it with the same spine: who is it for, what does it do for them that nothing else does, where does it still hurt, what would you ship, how would you know it worked. Have one product genuinely prepared this way. Pick one you actually use, ideally one adjacent to the company's problem space — and never pick the interviewer's own product unless you are ready to criticise it to their face.

05. The spine: how to spend the 35 minutes

A 45-minute slot is about 35 minutes of real work once you subtract introductions and their questions at the end. Here is the split that survives contact with a real interviewer.

The tell of a trained candidate is that they manage the clock out loud. "That's about five minutes on framing — I'll move to picking a segment now." It sounds mechanical written down; in the room it reads as control.

06. Step 1 · Frame — clarify, assume, plan

The prompt is deliberately under-specified. It is a test, not an oversight. What you do in the first 90 seconds sets your ceiling.

Take the pause

Say: "Give me thirty seconds to structure this." Then actually take it, and write. Interviewers do not penalise the silence — they penalise the rambling that happens when you skip it. A candidate who thinks first and speaks second sounds two levels more senior than one who narrates their own confusion.

Ask clarifying questions — but only the ones that change your answer

The advice "ask lots of questions" is half-right and gets misapplied. Ask questions whose answers would change what you do next. "What's the business goal — growth, retention, or revenue?" changes everything. "How many users does it have?" usually changes nothing. Two or three good ones, then move.

State your assumptions, then commit

Where you don't get an answer, assume out loud and move on: "I'll assume we're talking about the mobile app, in the US, and that we care about retention rather than new signups. Shout if you'd rather I went elsewhere." This is the move that separates senior from mid. Mid-level candidates wait to be told. Senior candidates make a defensible call, name it as a call, and keep moving.

the opening 90 seconds — a script you can adapt
# 1. Take the pause. Out loud, so it isn't dead air.
"Let me take thirty seconds to structure this."

# 2. Two or three questions that would actually change your answer.

# 4. Give them the map. This is the highest-leverage sentence you say.
"Here's my plan: I'll spend two minutes on who this product is really

07. Step 2 · Mission — why does this thing exist?

Four minutes. No more. You are establishing the yardstick you will use to justify every later choice.

Two moves. First, say what the product does for a human, not what it is. Not "it's a photo-sharing app" but "it lets people stay close to the people they love when they can't be in the room." Second, place it strategically: who else is trying to win this, and what does this company have that the others don't — the graph, the distribution, the data, the model?

Then state a mission sentence, because you will use it as a weapon later: when you reject a solution, you reject it against the mission. That is what makes a rejection sound like judgment instead of taste.

The most common way strong engineers lose this round

They spend fifteen minutes here. Mission and market feel like the "smart" part, so people luxuriate in it — and then sprint through segmentation, hand-wave the pain, and never reach a V1. The interviewer's notes say "never got to a product." Four minutes. Then leave.

08. Step 3 · Segment — choose one person

List the users, split them by motivation rather than demographics, then pick one and say why. This is the step most candidates think they did and did not.

Bad segmentation splits by attributes that don't change behaviour: "18–24s, 25–40s, over-40s." Good segmentation splits by what someone is trying to get done: for a food-delivery app — "the person feeding a family on a schedule," "the person who forgot to eat and wants food in 20 minutes," "the person treating themselves on a Friday." Those three want genuinely different products. Age doesn't.

Then pick one, using reach against how badly they're served today, and name a person: "I'm going with Maya, 34, two kids, cooks four nights a week, has about eleven minutes between getting home and someone crying." Specificity here pays for itself for the rest of the interview, because now every later decision has something to be tested against.

09. Step 4 · Pain — find the obstacle, not the wish

Walk your person through the journey, out loud, step by step. Then name where it hurts, and rank the hurts.

The distinction that earns points here: a need is a want; a problem is an obstacle. "I want dinner ideas" is a need — vague, unactionable. "By 6pm I've already decided forty things and I can't face deciding one more" is a problem: it has a cause, a moment, and an emotional charge. You can build against it. You cannot build against a wish.

Rank the pains on frequency against severity, and then choose. Interviewers are listening for the choice. "These are all important" is the sound of someone who cannot prioritise, which is the sound of someone who will not be able to run a project.

10. Step 5 · Solutions and the V1

Generate three genuinely different solutions — not three flavours of the same one. Then cut to a V1 you could actually ship this quarter.

"Genuinely different" means they attack the problem from different angles. For Maya's decision fatigue: (a) remove the decision — a weekly auto-plan she can veto; (b) shrink the decision — three options, never twelve; (c) move the decision — decide on Sunday when she has slack, not at 6pm when she doesn't. Those are three different products. "Add a recommendations tab" and "add a recommendations carousel" are one product twice.

Then commit to a V1 and say what you are deliberately leaving out. That sentence — "I'd cut the social features entirely for v1, because they serve a different segment" — is a seniority signal that almost nothing else replicates. Finish with the risk: what would make this fail, and what would you watch to find out early?

11. Metrics: north star, drivers, guardrails

Whatever your role, you will be asked "how would you know it worked?" Answering with a list of six metrics is a fail. Answering with one metric and no guardrail is also a fail.

The classic trap, and it comes up constantly: you are asked to measure a change to driver tipping in a rideshare app. Tips per trip is the obvious answer and the wrong one — you can raise it by nagging riders until they cancel, and you'd never see the damage. Earnings per online hour is the right shape: it captures the value the driver actually cares about, and it can't be won by cannibalising fares or supply. Then you name the guardrails — cancellation rate, rider satisfaction — that would tell you if you were cheating.

Say the sentence: "and I'd hold X and Y as guardrails, because the cheap way to win this metric is to hurt them." It is the single highest-leverage sentence in the metrics half of the interview.

12. The metric-drop playbook

"DAU dropped 5% week over week. Why?" This is its own question type, it has a canonical structure, and if you have the structure you will out-perform people with far more product experience.

Two things separate a strong answer here. First, rule out the instrument before you theorise about the world. A logging change, a broken pipeline, a bot farm that left — these are common, they're fast to check, and a candidate who checks them first is telling the interviewer they have actually been on call. Second, never accept the aggregate. A flat 5% drop is almost never flat underneath: it's usually −30% on Android in one country, and 0 everywhere else. Cut by platform, geography, tenure, and new-versus-existing before you say a single word about causes.

13. AI product sense — the round that is spreading fastest

This is the newest and, for engineers, the most consequential. At OpenAI, Anthropic and DeepMind it isn't a round — it's in every round. Meta added a dedicated Product Sense with AI interview for IC6+. Figma runs one. The questions look like: "How would you 10x Claude Code's weekly actives?" or "How would you double ChatGPT image creation with three engineers?"

Here is the thing the generic advice misses, and it's why engineers can win this round: you cannot CIRCLES your way through an AI question. The frameworks assume a deterministic system. The moment the feature is a model, four engineering questions become product questions, and they are exactly the questions you are already qualified to answer:

1
What happens when it's wrong? Not if — the model will be wrong some percentage of the time. Is that a shrug, or is it a lawsuit? A wrong autocomplete costs nothing. A wrong medication dosage costs everything. Your product design is your answer to this: confirmation steps, undo, showing sources, degrading to a human. Say this and you have separated yourself from most candidates in the room.
2
How would you evaluate it? "We'd test it" is a non-answer. What's the eval set, who labels it, what's the pass rate you'd ship at, and what's the offline-to-online gap? Building the eval before the feature is the AI-native instinct, and interviewers listen for it.
3
What does it cost, per call? Tokens, latency, GPU. A feature that costs $0.40 per user per day and is used ten times a day has a business model problem, not an engineering problem. Senior candidates cost their own idea unprompted.
4
Why is this a model, and not a rule? The killer follow-up. If regex or a lookup table gets you 90% of the way, the model is a liability — more cost, more latency, less predictability. Knowing when not to use AI is now a senior signal in itself.

The unfair advantage you already have

PMs walk into the AI product sense round and talk about the market. Engineers can walk in and say: "The capability is retrieval over the user's own repo. The eval set is 200 real questions with known answers, and I'd ship at 85% top-1. It costs about a cent a query, so I'd cache aggressively. And when it's wrong, it shows the file it read — so the user catches it instead of trusting it." That answer is unbeatable, and it is made of things you already know. Your job in prep is not to learn product management. It is to learn to say what you already know in product language.

14. What "senior" actually sounds like

The same answer, delivered two ways, gets two different levels. The difference is almost never the idea.

Concretely, the three senior signals interviewers write down:

1
You narrow before you're comfortable. Mid-level candidates keep options open because closing them feels risky. Senior candidates close them early, out loud, with a reason — and revisit only if the interviewer pushes.
2
You say what you're not doing. Naming the cut is worth more than naming the feature. It proves you know the cost of what you chose.
3
You argue with the question. Carefully. "You asked me to increase shares, but I think shares are a proxy — if we win them the way that's easiest, we'll hurt trust. Can I optimise for X instead?" Done well, this is the strongest thing you can do in the room. Done badly, it's arrogance. The difference is whether you offer an alternative and then do the work anyway.

15. If you're a data engineer

You will get the metrics variant, and you have an advantage you are probably not using.

Your instinct — "what does this number actually mean, and can I trust it?" — is product sense. When you ask whether DAU counts a user who opened the app and bounced in 400ms, you are doing exactly what this round rewards. The failure mode for data engineers is not lacking judgment; it's staying in the pipeline and never coming up to the user.

So make these moves explicitly:

1
Define the metric before you defend it. "Active" — over what window, counting what event, deduped how? Interviewers love this because most candidates skip it.
2
Question the instrument first. You've debugged a dashboard that lied. Say so. "Before I theorise, I'd check whether we changed the event schema last Tuesday" is a sentence a PM cannot say with authority and you can.
3
Then get to the human. Don't stop at "the pipeline is fine." Finish the sentence: "…so it's real, and it's Android users in Brazil on the pre-paid plan — which suggests the new video autoplay is eating their data." That last step is the one that gets you the offer.

And when they ask you to design something: your pitch is that you can see the data product nobody else can. The internal tool nobody scoped. The metric that's lying to the whole company. The pipeline whose 6-hour latency is quietly costing the sales team a day. That is product sense with a data engineer's eyes, and it is genuinely rare.

16. If you're an AI engineer

The AI product sense round is the one where your day job is the answer — but only if you translate it.

You know that a demo working three times in a row means nothing. You know that the gap between a great demo and a shippable feature is the long tail. You know what it costs to serve a 100k-context request. Every one of those is a product argument, and almost nobody else in the pipeline can make it.

The translation you need to practise is from engineering fact to user consequence:

What you'd normally sayWhat wins the round
"Latency is about 4 seconds.""Four seconds is past the point where people alt-tab away — so either I make it stream, or I make the wait feel like work being done."
"It hallucinates sometimes.""It's wrong maybe 8% of the time, and the user can't tell which 8%. So the product has to show its sources — otherwise we're training people to trust something unreliable."
"We'd need to fine-tune.""I'd ship the prompt version first to find out whether anyone wants it. Fine-tuning is a bet I'd only make once the eval set tells me the ceiling is real."
"It costs 3 cents a call.""At three cents and ten calls a day, this feature costs more per user than we charge. So either it drives conversion, or it's a paid tier."

The one that gets AI engineers rejected

Designing for the demo. You describe the happy path in loving detail — the agent plans, executes, self-corrects, delivers — and never say a word about what the user sees when it fails, loops, or confidently invents an answer. The interviewer's note reads: "no failure model." Reverse it: describe the wrong output first, then design the product around containing it. That single reordering makes you sound like someone who has shipped.

17. A worked example, end to end

The real prompt, from the AI labs: "How would you 10× the weekly active users of an AI coding tool?" Here is the whole spine, compressed — read it as a transcript of what good sounds like.

transcript — the shape of a strong 35 minutes
FRAME (2 min)
"Thirty seconds to structure. — Two questions: is 10x a real target or a
 provocation? I'll treat it as 'find a step change, not an optimisation.'
 And are we growing users, or usage per user? I'll assume users.
 Plan: who isn't using it and why, pick the biggest locked door, open it,
 then say how I'd know."

MISSION (3 min)
"The tool exists to let a developer stay in flow — to delegate the parts of
 the work that are typing, not thinking. It wins where it has context nobody
 else has: your repo, your history, your conventions. That's the moat, and
 it should constrain everything I pick."

SEGMENT (8 min)
"Three groups, split by why they're not here:
   1. Never tried it   — doesn't believe an LLM can handle a real codebase
   2. Tried, bounced   — it was wrong once on day one and they lost trust
   3. Uses it daily    — already converted, not where 10x lives

 10x cannot come from group 3. Group 1 is a marketing problem, and I don't
 have 10x of marketing. So: group 2 — the bounced. They installed it. They
 have the intent. Something specific broke, and that's addressable."

PAIN (8 min)
"Their journey: install → point it at a real repo → ask for something real
 → it confidently edits the wrong file → they revert → they never open it
 again. The pain isn't 'it's not smart enough.' It's:
   one bad edit destroys trust permanently, and the tool never earns it back.
 Frequency: happens on day one, to nearly everyone. Severity: terminal —
 they don't come back. That's the one."

SOLUTION (8 min)
"Three genuinely different attacks:
   a. Make it right more often  — better retrieval. Real, slow, everyone's doing it.
   b. Make being wrong cheap    — never let it write without a diff the user
                                  accepts. Wrongness becomes a suggestion, not damage.
   c. Make it earn trust slowly — start it read-only. It explains your code
                                  before it ever touches it.

 V1 is (b) — it's the smallest thing that changes the outcome. Cost of a
 mistake goes from 'reverted my work' to 'I said no.' I'd explicitly NOT
 build (a) for v1: it's a six-month bet and it doesn't fix the trust cliff,
 it just moves it.

 Risk: diffs add friction for the power users in group 3 — so it's default-on
 for new users and switchable off. I'd watch group 3's usage as a guardrail."

METRIC (3 min)
"North star: weekly active developers. But the metric that tells me if I'm
 right is narrower: day-1 to week-2 retention of new installs — because my
 whole thesis is that we're leaking at the trust cliff.
 Guardrails: edits accepted per session (did I make it annoying?), and
 daily usage of group 3 (did I tax the people who already love it?)."

Notice what that answer does not do. It doesn't list ten features. It doesn't try to be clever. It picks one user, one moment, one fix, says what it's sacrificing, and names the number that would prove it wrong. That is the whole game.

19. A three-week prep plan

This round rewards reps far more than reading. You cannot think your way to fluency; you have to talk your way there, badly, several times.

1
Week 1 — build the spine. Learn the five steps until you can recite them cold. Then do five questions out loud, on a timer, recorded. Play them back. It will be worse than you think — that's the point, and it is the single highest-value hour of the whole plan.
2
Week 2 — reps and range. One question a day, alternating types: design, improve, strategy, metric-drop, AI. Keep a one-page log per question: what segment did I pick, what pain, what V1, what did I cut. You're building a library of moves, not answers.
3
Week 3 — pressure and polish. Two mock interviews with a human who will interrupt you (that's the part you can't simulate alone). Then prepare your three anchors: the product you know cold, a project of yours told as a product story, and — for AI roles — a crisp opinion on where models are actually useful and where they are theatre.

A question bank to work through

practice set — say every one of these out loud, on a clock

Glossary

Product sense
Judgment about which problem is worth solving, for whom, and what to build first — under ambiguity.
North star metric
The single number that best represents the value users get. Moves slowly; everything else should ladder up to it.
Input driver
A metric a team can actually move this quarter (adoption, frequency, yield) that feeds the north star.
Guardrail metric
A number that must not get worse while you chase the north star. Exists to catch you winning dishonestly.
Counter-metric
A guardrail chosen specifically because it captures the cheapest way to game your primary metric.
Segment
A group of users who share a motivation, not a demographic. If two segments would want the same product, they're one segment.
Persona
One named, specific human standing in for the segment, so later decisions have something concrete to be tested against.
Need vs problem
A need is a wish ("I want it faster"). A problem is an obstacle with a cause and a moment ("it times out while I'm on the train"). You can only build against problems.
V1 / MVP
The smallest thing you could ship that would still solve the prioritised pain — and prove or kill the thesis.
Eval set
A fixed set of labelled examples used to measure an AI feature's quality as a rate. The AI-native replacement for "it works."
Failure model
The explicit answer to "what does the user see when the model is wrong?" Its absence is the most common AI-round rejection.
Offline–online gap
When a model improves on your eval set but the business metric doesn't move. Usually means the eval doesn't reflect real use.
CIRCLES
A popular PM answer framework (Comprehend, Identify, Report, Cut, List, Evaluate, Summarise). Useful scaffolding for design questions; breaks down on AI questions, where the system is probabilistic.
